CCR Facility · A worked example

How a small guarantee unlocks long-term finance in frontier markets

Follow one company in Kenya from start to finish: the financing it needs, the currency problem it hits, the swap that solves it, and the guarantee that makes the whole thing possible. No finance background required.

01 The company

A company that cannot get the right kind of loan

Picture a solar-power company in Kenya. It builds and runs a plant that sells electricity to local households and businesses, and it is paid in Kenyan shillings. The shilling stands in here for the local currencies across the frontier markets the Facility is built for.

It needs USD 100m to build its plant, and it needs that money for about ten years. Local lenders cannot provide a sum that large for that long, so the only financing available at the right scale and tenor is a loan in US dollars. The company borrows USD 100m, even though every unit of its income is earned in shillings.

02 The problem

Income in one currency, debt in another

The company earns shillings but must repay dollars. While the exchange rate holds steady that is manageable, but exchange rates move, and frontier currencies tend to weaken over time.

The same USD 100m debt, valued in shillings. A move from 120 to 160 grows it by a third, with nothing about the business changed.

A large enough move can turn a profitable project into one that cannot pay, purely because of the exchange rate. This currency mismatch, rather than the quality of the underlying projects, is the single biggest reason that local-currency businesses in frontier markets struggle to raise long-term finance.

Where does the risk go?

Currency risk does not vanish when no one carries it deliberately. In a power project the dollar exposure starts with the producer, but it rarely stops there. Power purchase agreements and utilities are built to pass it down the chain, and it ends up where it usually does: on the customer’s bill.

In Kenya this is explicit. Every electricity bill carries a forex adjustment, a per-unit surcharge reset every month that recovers the exchange-rate losses producers take on their dollar PPAs and loans. Recently it has run between about KES 0.72 and KES 1.23 per unit. When the shilling weakens, the line climbs, and households and businesses pay more for the very same power.

That surcharge is the same dollar exposure from the start of the story, passed all the way down. Hedging it upstream, which is exactly what the swap and the guarantee make possible, is what keeps it off the bill.

03 The fix

A cross-currency swap

The company already has a dollar loan. A cross-currency swap is a second contract laid on top of it: a hedge, with the loan as the hedged item. Under the swap the provider takes on the company’s dollar payments, and in return the company pays the provider in shillings, at a rate fixed today.

The clearest way to understand it is to step through the life of the swap and watch the payments move.

How the swap works, step by step

Click through each stage. The top row is what the company pays; the bottom row is what it receives.

At the start. The company draws its USD 100m loan from the lender and, the same day, swaps it with the provider for KES 12bn to build the plant. The borrowed dollars pass straight through.

Add the swap to the loan and the dollar legs cancel: the provider hands the company exactly the dollars its loan owes. What is left is a fixed shilling payment, a synthetic shilling loan that matches the company’s income, so the exchange rate no longer changes what the company pays. The currency risk has not gone, though. It has moved onto the provider’s books, which is where the next question begins.

04 The catch

The swap creates a new exposure

The risk has not vanished. It has moved to the provider. For most of the time that is simply its business, and it earns a fee for it. The difficulty arises if the company defaults partway through the ten years.

At any moment a swap has a value: the amount it would cost to replace it at current market prices. This running value is what finance people call the mark-to-market, often shortened to MTM. At the start the swap is worth nothing to either side. As exchange rates move it gains value for one party and loses exactly the same value for the other.

If the company defaults at a moment when the swap is valuable to the provider, the provider is left with a loss it cannot recover from a failed counterparty. That uncollectible amount is the exposure at the heart of this story, and it is exactly what the CCR Facility is designed to cover.

05 The risk

Which way does the risk run?

Here is the part that makes the whole structure work. The provider receives the shilling payments and makes the dollar payments. The shillings it receives are worth more in dollars when the shilling is strong, and less when it is weak. So the swap is valuable to the provider, and the provider is exposed, only when the shilling has strengthened.

06 Why it is safe

The risk corrects itself

Now combine that with the question of when the company is actually likely to default. It earns shillings and owes dollars, so it is under most pressure when the shilling is weak, because that is when its dollar costs are highest relative to its income. It is comfortable when the shilling is strong, because its dollar costs are then cheap.

Put the two facts side by side. The provider is exposed only when the shilling is strong, which is precisely when the company is healthy and least likely to fail. When the shilling is weak and the company is in danger, the exposure is zero. The big-loss case and the high-risk case sit at opposite ends.

The two move in opposite directions. The guarantee is only needed on the right, where the company is safe. The two rarely happen together, which is what makes it cheap and safe to provide.

A guarantee that only ever pays out when the company is healthy is far safer, and far cheaper to provide, than the headline exposure figures suggest. The peak exposure might reach USD 20m to 40m, but a payout requires the rare combination of a strong currency and a default at the same time. Finance people call this favourable alignment right-way risk; the plain point is that the structure is self-correcting.

07 The Facility

Where the CCR Facility fits

This is the gap the Facility fills. Providers have the capacity to offer cross-currency swaps, but they will not extend them to frontier-market borrowers for ten years while carrying the default exposure unprotected. The CCR Facility issues a guarantee covering exactly that exposure: if the company defaults while the swap is valuable to the provider, the Facility makes the provider whole.

The swap with the guarantee added. The provider carries the exposure on the company; the CCR Facility stands behind it.

Because the risk is self-correcting, the Facility can offer this protection at a modest price. For the example transaction the guarantee breaks even at around 32 basis points a year on the amount covered, roughly a third of one percent, and is priced in the range of 35 to 45 basis points, a level the provider is comfortable with while still leaving the Facility a margin.

→ The development payoff

One small piece of credit support unlocks the whole chain

Guarantee the exposure, and the swap becomes available. Make the swap available, and the company can borrow safely in the currency it earns. And with that, a sound local-currency project that the market would otherwise leave unfinanced can be built.

The guarantee is the small piece of credit support that unlocks everything upstream of it.
